Visualization Write-Up

Research Question

How does projected surface temperature change vary across the globe under different emission scenarios, and how do regional trends diverge over time? This question matters because different parts of the world face sharply different warming trajectories. Our EDA revealed that warming is deeply uneven: polar regions amplify 2–4× faster than the global mean, continental interiors heat faster than coastal zones, and densely populated regions like South Asia and the Sahel face disproportionate warming relative to their emissions. The gap between SSP2-4.5 and SSP5-8.5 compounds over decades, making the choice of emission pathway a critical variable for any region.

Emission Scenarios Explained

SSP (Shared Socioeconomic Pathway) is the IPCC AR6 framework for modeling how human choices about energy, land use, and policy shape future greenhouse gas concentrations. Five numbered pathways exist — SSP1 through SSP5 — spanning a range from aggressive climate action to unchecked fossil-fuel growth:

  • SSP1-1.9 / SSP1-2.6 (Very low): Strong global cooperation, rapid decarbonization, consistent with limiting warming to 1.5–2°C. This pathway is not included in this dataset but represents the Paris Agreement target.
  • SSP2-4.5 (Moderate — shown in blue): "Middle of the road" — partial climate policy, CO₂ emissions peak around mid-century and stabilize. Radiative forcing reaches ~4.5 W/m² by 2100, implying roughly +2.7°C of global warming above pre-industrial levels.
  • SSP3-7.0 (High): Regional rivalry, fragmented policy, high land-use emissions. Not in this dataset.
  • SSP5-8.5 (Very high — shown in red): Fossil-fuel intensive development with no effective climate policy; CO₂ roughly doubles by 2100. Radiative forcing reaches ~8.5 W/m², implying ~4.4°C of global warming — the worst-case baseline used in most headline risk assessments.

This visualization focuses on SSP2 and SSP5 because they represent the practical range between moderate action and inaction — the two most policy-relevant bookends of the spectrum.

Regional Warming Patterns

Beyond the well-known polar amplification signal, our data reveals several other notable regional patterns worth exploring with the comparison tool:

  • Polar amplification (Russia, Canada, Greenland): Arctic land masses warm 2–4× faster than the global average by 2080 under SSP5-8.5, driven by sea-ice albedo loss and changes in atmospheric circulation. Russia ranks #1 among all countries in relative warming in our dataset.
  • Continental interior drying (Central Asia, Sahel, inland Australia): These regions warm faster than nearby coastal areas because they lack the moderating effect of ocean heat absorption. Kazakhstan, Mongolia, and Sudan show above-average warming trends.
  • Tropical amplification under high emissions (India, Nigeria, Brazil): While tropical regions warm more slowly in absolute terms than the poles, their baseline temperatures are already near human physiological limits. Even modest additional warming (+1–2°C) has outsized impacts on heat stress, agriculture, and monsoon patterns.
  • Coastal moderation (Indonesia, New Zealand, United Kingdom): Ocean proximity buffers warming, causing these countries to track at or slightly below the global average — which can still be misleading since sea-level rise is the dominant climate risk for coastal nations.
  • Major emitter comparison (USA vs China): These two countries are responsible for the majority of cumulative historical emissions, yet their projected warming trajectories are shaped by their geography as much as their emissions — comparing them illustrates how the same global forcing affects different climates differently.

Design Rationale & Encoding Choices

  • Natural Earth projection (map): Chosen for its area-preserving quality at mid-latitudes and familiar appearance. Mercator was rejected because it severely exaggerates polar regions — the very areas of greatest warming — creating a misleading visual emphasis.
  • Diverging blue→red color scale (map): Centered at 0°C (freezing point), this physically meaningful anchor immediately conveys below-vs-above freezing. The palette matches the perceptual convention in climate science. A sequential scale was rejected because it would hide the meaningful zero crossing.
  • Canvas raster beneath SVG borders: Climate model output is gridded data, not country-level data. Rendering each grid cell directly preserves spatial fidelity and reveals sub-national gradients — e.g., the Tibetan Plateau vs. the Ganges plain within India, or continental vs. coastal gradients across Australia.
  • Relative anomaly chart (right panel): When a country is selected, the Y-axis switches from absolute temperature to warming relative to the global average (Δ vs 2020, minus global Δ). This makes it immediately clear whether a region is warming faster or slower than the world — the key comparative insight. The country comparison mode extends this to two countries simultaneously.
  • Decade slider + play button: The slider gives direct control over the temporal comparison; the play button enables passive animation at three speeds for presentation contexts.
  • Suggested comparisons presets: Four curated country pairs (Russia/Canada, India/Nigeria, USA/China, Brazil/Indonesia) guide the reader toward the most instructive regional contrasts, making the comparison feature immediately discoverable.

Data Transformations

The CMIP6 dataset was subsetted to Near-Surface Air Temperature (tas) for SSP2-4.5 and SSP5-8.5 scenarios, 2015–2100. The spatial file contains decadal means on a ~1.25° × 0.94° global grid (~55,000 cells); multiple ensemble members per cell are averaged to reduce model noise. The timeseries file contains global area-weighted annual means. Temperature is displayed in °C; the full spatial range (−52°C to +40°C) is preserved. Color amplification (3×) is applied relative to the 2020 baseline for map display only — tooltips and the chart always show true values.

Development Process

This is a group submission for DSC 106 (Nikitha Kerudi, Yohan Kwon, Kyle Duong). Approximate time breakdown:

  • Data acquisition and Python preprocessing (xarray, pandas, Google Colab): ~3 hrs
  • Exploratory analysis in matplotlib (6 static figures): ~2 hrs
  • D3.js map: projection, canvas raster, SVG border overlay, coordinate transforms: ~7 hrs
  • Time series chart: axes, global lines, crosshair, relative anomaly mode, dynamic Y-scale: ~5 hrs
  • Controls, cross-panel linking, tooltips, play/pause animation, styling: ~4 hrs
  • Country comparison mode, rank badges, search box, suggested comparisons: ~4 hrs
  • Write-up: ~1.5 hrs

The most technically complex aspect was the smooth year-by-year map interpolation — linearly blending between four decadal temperature grids per frame using pre-computed projected pixel positions and a 512-entry color LUT, achieving real-time performance across 55,000+ grid cells.
